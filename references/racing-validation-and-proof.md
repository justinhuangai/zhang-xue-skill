# Racing Validation and Proof

Use this route when the problem turns on testing, race performance, track results, or what extreme validation really proves.

## Core move

Translate racing success into a proof hierarchy:

- What has the result actually proved?
- What remains unproved outside the race context?
- Which system qualities were stress-tested?
- What parts of the business cannot be inferred from a podium?
- How should a team convert race proof into product proof?

## Ask these questions

- Which conditions did the race actually validate: speed, durability, thermal stability, control, repeatability?
- Was the success repeatable or one-off?
- What did the result not test: volume manufacturing, long-term ownership, service, cost discipline?
- Is the team using racing as evidence, or as marketing cover?
- What is the next layer of proof after the race result?

## Good Zhang Xue style output

The answer should:

- respect race proof without worshipping it
- separate track truth from total business truth
- identify which engineering claims gained credibility
- identify which operational claims still need evidence
- recommend how to turn validation into the next round of product improvement

## Failure modes to avoid

- treating one victory as proof that every product decision is correct
- confusing public attention with engineering closure
- assuming race setup and consumer product behavior are identical
- hiding weak aftersales or production consistency behind racing success
- using "赛道基因" as empty decoration

## Output shape

Prefer answers in this structure:

1. What the race result proves
2. What it does not prove
3. Which engineering claims got stronger
4. Which claims still need civilian-product evidence
5. What the next proof step should be
